\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================================
Continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) evaluate sample densities by integrating the
divergence of a learned vector field along the flow trajectory. In practice, this
divergence is estimated stochastically via the Hutchinson trace estimator, which
introduces $O(d)$ variance and requires multiple random projections for accuracy.
We observe that the Nos\'{e}--Hoover thermostat --- a deterministic ODE from
statistical mechanics that samples Boltzmann distributions --- possesses an
analytically known divergence: $\divergence(f) = -d \cdot g(\xi)$, where $\xi$ is
a scalar thermostat variable and $g$ is the coupling function. This gives $O(1)$
cost and \emph{exactly zero variance} for log-density tracking. We introduce
NH-CNF, which augments the state with momentum and a thermostat variable, evolves
the extended system under Nos\'{e}--Hoover dynamics, and accumulates log-densities
exactly. On multimodal 2D targets, NH-CNF achieves up to 6$\times$ better energy
distance than Langevin dynamics, with the strongest gains on targets with complex
topology (spirals, well-separated modes) and modest gains on simpler targets. Its zero-variance density evaluation contrasts
sharply with Hutchinson's estimator, whose variance grows linearly with dimension.
On Bayesian neural network posteriors, NH-CNF produces 98\% calibrated uncertainty
estimates. We also report an honest limitation: deterministic NH dynamics suffer
mode trapping at $d > 20$, a manifestation of the KAM theorem. The NH-CNF
framework bridges a 40-year-old thermostat equation with modern generative
modeling, providing the first CNF with provably exact divergence from physical
principles.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================================

Continuous normalizing flows (CNFs) define a generative model by specifying a
vector field $f(z, t)$ and integrating the ordinary differential equation
$\dd z/\dd t = f(z, t)$ to transform a simple base distribution into a complex
target \citep{chen2018neural}. The change of log-density along the flow is
governed by the instantaneous change-of-variables formula:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\dd}{\dd t} \log p_t(z_t) = -\tr\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right)
  = -\divergence(f).
  \label{eq:instantaneous_cov}
\end{equation}
Computing this divergence exactly requires $O(d)$ evaluations of the vector field
(one per diagonal element of the Jacobian), which is prohibitive for high-dimensional
problems. The standard workaround, introduced by FFJORD \citep{grathwohl2019ffjord},
is the Hutchinson trace estimator: $\tr(J) \approx v^\top J v$ where
$v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. This estimator is unbiased but introduces variance
that grows as $O(d)$ and accumulates over the integration trajectory.

We make a simple observation: the Nos\'{e}--Hoover (NH) thermostat
\citep{nose1984unified, hoover1985canonical}, a deterministic ODE system invented
in the 1980s to sample canonical distributions in molecular dynamics, has a vector
field whose divergence is \emph{analytically known} and costs $O(1)$ to evaluate.
The divergence depends only on a single scalar thermostat variable $\xi$, not on
the $d$-dimensional state. This property is well known in the molecular dynamics
community but, to our knowledge, has not been exploited for generative modeling.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{NH-CNF architecture.} We formulate the NH thermostat as a
    continuous normalizing flow on an augmented state space $(q, p, \xi)$, with
    exact, zero-variance log-density tracking (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{Exact divergence theorem.} We prove that
    $\divergence(f) = -d \cdot g(\xi)$ for the NH flow, where $g$ is the
    thermostat coupling function. The proof is elementary --- three lines of
    calculus --- but the consequence is significant: density evaluation is free
    (Section~\ref{sec:exact_div}).
  \item \textbf{Experimental validation.} NH-CNF achieves up to 6$\times$ better
    energy distance than Langevin dynamics on multimodal 2D targets (with the
    largest gains on complex topology), zero-variance
    log-likelihoods, and 98\% calibrated Bayesian neural network posteriors
    (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
  \item \textbf{Honest limitations.} We document the failure mode:
    deterministic NH dynamics lose mode coverage at $d > 20$ due to KAM-type
    trapping, a fundamental constraint of autonomous Hamiltonian-like flows
    (Section~\ref{sec:scaling}).
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:concept} illustrates the structural correspondence between
diffusion models and NH thermostats. Both transform noise into samples; the
difference is that the NH flow tracks density exactly through its thermostat
variable, while diffusion models require a learned score network.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Continuous Normalizing Flows}
\label{sec:cnf_background}

A CNF defines a diffeomorphism $\phi_t : \R^d \to \R^d$ by integrating the
neural ODE $\dd z/\dd t = f_\theta(z, t)$ from $t=0$ to $t=T$
\citep{chen2018neural}. The log-density of a sample $z_T = \phi_T(z_0)$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \log p_T(z_T) = \log p_0(z_0)
    - \int_0^T \divergence\bigl(f_\theta(z_t, t)\bigr)\, \dd t.
  \label{eq:cnf_logp}
\end{equation}
The computational bottleneck is the divergence integral. For a general neural
network $f_\theta$, the Jacobian $\partial f / \partial z \in \R^{d \times d}$
is dense and unstructured. FFJORD \citep{grathwohl2019ffjord} approximates the
trace using the Hutchinson estimator:
\begin{equation}
  \tr(J) = \E_{v}\bigl[v^\top J v\bigr], \quad v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I),
  \label{eq:hutchinson}
\end{equation}
which requires one Jacobian-vector product (JVP) per random vector $v$. The
variance of a single Hutchinson sample is $\Var[v^\top J v] = 2\|J\|_F^2$, which
scales as $O(d)$ for typical networks. Reducing this variance to acceptable levels
requires $n_v$ independent vectors, multiplying the cost by $n_v$.

\subsection{Nos\'{e}--Hoover Thermostat Dynamics}
\label{sec:nh_background}

The Nos\'{e}--Hoover thermostat \citep{nose1984unified, hoover1985canonical}
extends Hamiltonian dynamics with a scalar feedback variable $\xi$ that acts as a
``friction coefficient,'' driving the kinetic energy toward the canonical value.
The equations of motion on the extended phase space $(q, p, \xi) \in \R^d \times
\R^d \times \R$ are:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\dd q}{\dd t} &= \frac{p}{m}, \label{eq:nh_q} \\
  \frac{\dd p}{\dd t} &= -\nabla V(q) - g(\xi)\, p, \label{eq:nh_p} \\
  \frac{\dd \xi}{\dd t} &= \frac{1}{Q}\left(\frac{|p|^2}{m} - d\,\kT\right),
  \label{eq:nh_xi}
\end{align}
where $V(q)$ is the potential energy, $m$ is the mass (set to 1 throughout),
$Q > 0$ is the thermostat mass controlling coupling strength, and $g(\xi)$ is a
coupling function satisfying $g(0) = 0$ and $g'(\xi) \geq 0$. The classical
choice is $g(\xi) = \xi$; we use $g(\xi) = \tanh(\xi)$, which provides bounded
friction and improved numerical stability. The specific form of $g$ is largely
irrelevant to sampling quality: what matters is the effective thermostat mass
$Q_{\mathrm{eff}} = Q / g'(0)$ \citep{hoover1985canonical}.

The NH dynamics preserve the extended invariant measure:
\begin{equation}
  \rho(q, p, \xi) \propto
    \exp\!\left(-\frac{H(q,p)}{\kT} - \frac{Q\,\Phi(\xi)}{\kT}\right),
  \label{eq:nh_invariant}
\end{equation}
where $H(q,p) = |p|^2/(2m) + V(q)$ and $\Phi(\xi) = \int_0^\xi g(s)\,\dd s$.
Marginalizing over $p$ and $\xi$ yields the canonical distribution
$p(q) \propto \exp(-V(q)/\kT)$ on the position variables --- exactly the target
distribution for sampling.

\paragraph{Historical context.}
Nos\'{e} \citep{nose1984unified} introduced the extended Lagrangian formulation;
Hoover \citep{hoover1985canonical} derived the real-time equations of motion.
Martyna et al.\ \citep{martyna1992nose} introduced Nos\'{e}--Hoover chains (NHC)
to improve ergodicity by coupling multiple thermostat variables in series. The
connection to colored-noise thermostats and generalized Langevin equations was
established by Ceriotti et al.\ \citep{ceriotti2010colored}.

\section{NH-CNF: Thermostat Flows with Exact Divergence}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{The Exact Divergence Property}
\label{sec:exact_div}

The central theoretical observation is that the NH vector field
$f(q, p, \xi) = (\dot{q}, \dot{p}, \dot{\xi})$ defined by
Equations~\eqref{eq:nh_q}--\eqref{eq:nh_xi} has an analytically known divergence.

\begin{theorem}[Exact divergence of the NH flow]
\label{thm:exact_div}
The divergence of the Nos\'{e}--Hoover vector field on the extended phase space
$(q, p, \xi) \in \R^d \times \R^d \times \R$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \divergence(f)
    = \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial \dot{q}_i}{\partial q_i}
    + \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial \dot{p}_i}{\partial p_i}
    + \frac{\partial \dot{\xi}}{\partial \xi}
    = -d \cdot g(\xi).
  \label{eq:exact_div}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We compute each block of the divergence separately.

\textit{Position block.}
$\dot{q}_i = p_i / m$ depends only on $p_i$, not on $q_i$.
Therefore $\partial \dot{q}_i / \partial q_i = 0$ for all $i$, and the position
contribution is zero.

\textit{Momentum block.}
$\dot{p}_i = -\partial V / \partial q_i - g(\xi)\, p_i$. Taking the derivative
with respect to $p_i$:
\[
  \frac{\partial \dot{p}_i}{\partial p_i} = -g(\xi).
\]
This holds for each $i = 1, \ldots, d$, so the momentum contribution is
$\sum_i (-g(\xi)) = -d \cdot g(\xi)$.

\textit{Thermostat block.}
$\dot{\xi} = (|p|^2/m - d\,\kT) / Q$. Since $|p|^2$ depends on $p$, not on
$\xi$, we have $\partial \dot{\xi} / \partial \xi = 0$.

Summing the three contributions gives
$\divergence(f) = 0 + (-d \cdot g(\xi)) + 0 = -d \cdot g(\xi)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The divergence depends only on the scalar $\xi$ through the function $g$.
Evaluating it costs $O(1)$ --- a single function evaluation of $g(\xi)$ ---
regardless of the state dimension $d$. This is in stark contrast to the
Hutchinson estimator, which requires at least one $O(d)$ JVP per divergence
evaluation. Moreover, the NH divergence has \emph{exactly zero variance}: it is a
deterministic function of the current state, not a stochastic estimate.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Physical interpretation.}
The NH flow is \emph{conformal-symplectic}: the phase-space volume element
contracts or expands at a rate determined entirely by the scalar thermostat
channel $\xi$. When $g(\xi) > 0$ (positive friction), the flow is contracting;
when $g(\xi) < 0$ (negative friction, i.e., the system is too cold), the flow
expands. The thermostat variable $\xi$ acts as a one-dimensional ``cheap score''
that encodes the system's deviation from thermal equilibrium --- replacing the
$d$-dimensional score $\nabla \log p$ that a diffusion model would learn.

\subsection{The NH-CNF Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}

Given a target distribution $p_{\mathrm{target}}(q) \propto \exp(-V(q))$ where
$V(q)$ is known (up to a constant), the NH-CNF proceeds as follows:

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{NH-CNF: Sampling and density evaluation}
\label{alg:nhcnf}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Target potential $V(q)$, temperature $\kT$, thermostat mass $Q$,
  coupling function $g$, integration steps $T$, step size $\Delta t$
\STATE \textbf{Initialize:} $q_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$ or warm-start from data;
  $p_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, m\kT \cdot I_d)$; $\xi_0 = 0$
\STATE $\log w \leftarrow 0$ \hfill\COMMENT{log-density accumulator}
\FOR{$n = 0, 1, \ldots, T-1$}
  \STATE Integrate $(q, p, \xi)$ one step via velocity Verlet splitting
  \STATE $\log w \leftarrow \log w + d \cdot g(\xi_n) \cdot \Delta t$
    \hfill\COMMENT{exact divergence integral}
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Output:} samples $q_T$ with log-weights $\log w$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The log-density of the output sample $q_T$ relative to the initial distribution is:
\begin{equation}
  \log p_T(q_T) = \log p_0(q_0) + \int_0^T d \cdot g\bigl(\xi(t)\bigr)\,\dd t,
  \label{eq:logp_tracking}
\end{equation}
where the integral is accumulated exactly (up to ODE integrator discretization
error) at each time step. No stochastic estimator is involved.

\subsection{Multi-Scale $Q$: The Thermostat Noise Schedule}
\label{sec:multiscale}

A single thermostat variable $\xi$ with mass $Q$ couples to the system at a
single characteristic frequency $\omega_Q \sim 1/\sqrt{Q}$. This is analogous to
a diffusion model with a single noise level --- it works for unimodal targets but
struggles with multi-scale structure.

We introduce $N$ parallel thermostat variables $\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_N$ with
masses $Q_1 < Q_2 < \cdots < Q_N$ spaced logarithmically:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\dd p}{\dd t} &= -\nabla V(q) - \Gamma(t)\, p, \quad
  \Gamma(t) = \sum_{k=1}^N g(\xi_k(t)), \label{eq:multi_p} \\
  \frac{\dd \xi_k}{\dd t} &= \frac{1}{Q_k}
    \left(\frac{|p|^2}{m} - d\,\kT\right), \quad k = 1, \ldots, N.
  \label{eq:multi_xi}
\end{align}
The divergence generalizes straightforwardly:
\begin{equation}
  \divergence(f) = -d \cdot \sum_{k=1}^N g(\xi_k),
  \label{eq:multi_div}
\end{equation}
which remains $O(N)$ --- independent of $d$ and still exact.

The multi-scale $Q$ spectrum plays the role of a noise schedule in diffusion
models: small $Q_k$ (strong coupling, high frequency) drives fast local
equilibration, while large $Q_k$ (weak coupling, low frequency) enables slow
global exploration. The crucial difference is that this ``schedule'' arises from
physical thermostat dynamics rather than a hand-designed or learned function.

\subsection{Connection to Diffusion Models}
\label{sec:diffusion_connection}

The structural correspondence between NH thermostats and score-based diffusion
models \citep{song2021score, ho2020denoising} is summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:correspondence}. Both frameworks transform noise into samples
through a time-dependent dynamical process. The key distinction is the trade-off
identified in the literature on autonomous vs.\ non-autonomous flows: the NH
thermostat achieves exact density tracking by extending the state space with
auxiliary variables $(p, \xi)$, while diffusion models operate in the original
space but require learning a time-dependent score function.

\begin{table}[t]
  \centering
  \caption{\textbf{Structural correspondence between diffusion models and NH
    thermostats.} Both frameworks generate samples by transforming noise through
    dynamics. The NH approach trades state augmentation for exact divergence.}
  \label{tab:correspondence}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Component} & \textbf{Diffusion model} & \textbf{NH thermostat} \\
    \midrule
    Noise schedule & $\beta(t)$ (learned/designed) & $Q$ spectrum (physical) \\
    Score function & $s_\theta(x, t) \approx \nabla \log p_t$ & $\xi(t)$ (1D scalar, ``cheap score'') \\
    Dynamics & SDE (stochastic) & ODE (deterministic) \\
    Density evaluation & Hutchinson, $O(d)$, stochastic & Exact, $O(1)$, deterministic \\
    State space & $\R^d$ (original) & $\R^{2d+N}$ (augmented) \\
    Training & Required (score matching) & Not required ($V$ given) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{The fundamental trade-off.}
NH-CNF operates in an augmented $(2d+N)$-dimensional space with an
\emph{autonomous} ODE (the vector field depends only on the current state, not
explicitly on time). Diffusion models operate in $d$ dimensions with a
\emph{non-autonomous} ODE/SDE (the score network is time-conditioned). The
autonomous structure is what enables the exact divergence: because the dynamics
are derived from a physical invariant measure, the phase-space geometry is
analytically tractable.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ============================================================================

We evaluate NH-CNF on five experiments designed to test distinct aspects of the
framework: sample quality (E1), divergence accuracy (E3), phase-space mechanism
(E5), Bayesian posterior calibration (E2), log-likelihood (E7), and dimension
scaling (E6). All experiments use $g(\xi) = \tanh(\xi)$, velocity Verlet
integration, and $\kT = 1$ unless stated otherwise.

\subsection{Sample Quality on 2D Targets}
\label{sec:e1}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We test NH-CNF on four standard 2D targets: two moons, two spirals, checkerboard,
and eight Gaussians. The potential $V(q)$ is fitted via kernel density estimation
on training data. NH-CNF uses multi-scale $Q = \{0.1, 1.0, 10.0\}$ with 3 chains
per $Q$ value (9 chains total), 100k steps per chain, 20\% burn-in, and thinning
every 50th sample (${\sim}$14k independent samples). The baseline is the
unadjusted Langevin algorithm (ULA) with step size $\epsilon = 0.005$ and 8000
steps. Quality is measured by energy distance (ED) to 10k ground-truth samples.
Note that NH-CNF produces more samples ($N{=}14{,}400$) than ULA ($N{=}1{,}600$)
due to its multi-chain, long-run design; the comparison reflects the
practical output of each method under reasonable computational budgets.

\subsection{Exact Divergence Advantage}
\label{sec:e3}

\paragraph{The variance problem.}
The Hutchinson estimator $\hat{\tr}(J) = v^\top J v$ is unbiased, but its
variance grows with dimension. For a single random vector $v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$,
the variance is $2\|J\|_F^2$. Over $T$ integration steps, the accumulated
log-density error has variance $\sim T \cdot \Var[\text{single step}]$, creating
what we call the \emph{Hutchinson horizon}: the trajectory length beyond which
estimator noise dominates ODE integrator error.

\paragraph{Quantitative comparison.}
We compare NH-CNF's exact divergence against Hutchinson with $n_v = 1$ and
$n_v = 5$ random vectors, across dimensions $d \in \{2, 10, 50, 100, 500\}$.
Table~\ref{tab:e3} reports the variance of 100 independent divergence evaluations
at the same state.

\subsection{Phase-Space Mechanism}
\label{sec:e5}

To build intuition for \emph{how} the NH-CNF works, we visualize the full

Panel~(d) is the key insight: the friction signal $g(\xi(t))$ that the thermostat
produces as a byproduct of maintaining thermal equilibrium is \emph{exactly} the
divergence signal that a CNF needs for density evaluation. No separate computation
is required --- the thermostat's feedback mechanism provides density tracking for
free.

\subsection{Dimension Scaling --- Honest Limitations}
\label{sec:scaling}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We test on a Gaussian mixture model (5 modes arranged in a ring) at dimensions
$d \in \{2, 5, 10, 20, 50\}$ using the exact GMM potential (not KDE). Mode
coverage is measured as the number of modes with $\geq 5\%$ of samples within
2 standard deviations of the mode center.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:e6} reports the results. NH-CNF maintains full mode coverage
through $d = 10$ but drops sharply to 1/5 modes at $d = 20$ and 0/5 at
$d = 50$. Langevin maintains full coverage through $d = 20$ and also fails at
$d = 50$. Notably, Langevin achieves lower energy distance at \emph{all}
dimensions tested with the GMM potential --- the NH-CNF advantage seen in
Section~\ref{sec:e1} is specific to the 2D KDE-potential setting with
warm-starting.

    NH-CNF degrades at $d \geq 20$ while Langevin remains stable.
    (b)~Mode coverage vs.\ dimension: NH-CNF visits all 5 modes at $d \leq 10$

  \label{fig:scaling}

\paragraph{Diagnosis.}
Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} reveals both the strength and limitation of NH-CNF. At
$d = 2$, NH-CNF visits all 5 modes. At $d = 20$, it collapses to a single mode
cluster, while Langevin maintains full coverage via stochastic exploration. The
energy distance (panel~a) confirms: NH-CNF degrades at $d \geq 20$ while
Langevin remains stable. This is the well-known KAM trapping phenomenon:
deterministic trajectories in high dimension get trapped near invariant
structures. Stochastic methods avoid this by continuously injecting noise that
breaks the invariant tori. This is not a tuning problem --- it is a structural
limitation of autonomous deterministic flows with a scalar thermostat variable.

\paragraph{Mitigation strategies (future work).}
Potential solutions include: (i)~periodic stochastic momentum refreshment (hybrid
deterministic-stochastic flows), (ii)~adaptive $Q$ schedules that respond to
mixing diagnostics, (iii)~vector thermostats with per-dimension $\xi_i$ variables
(at the cost of losing the $O(1)$ divergence property), and (iv)~Nos\'{e}--Hoover
chains with deeper thermostat hierarchies.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Continuous normalizing flows.}
Neural ODEs \citep{chen2018neural} established the framework for CNFs. FFJORD
\citep{grathwohl2019ffjord} introduced the Hutchinson trace estimator that made
CNFs scalable. OT-Flow \citep{onken2021ot} and flow matching
\citep{lipman2023flow} provide alternative training objectives. All of these use
learned vector fields with stochastic divergence estimation; NH-CNF provides a
physics-derived alternative with exact divergence for settings where $V(q)$ is
known.

\paragraph{Augmented flows.}
ANODE \citep{dupont2019augmented} showed that augmenting the state space with
extra dimensions improves expressiveness of neural ODEs. NH-CNF augments with
physically motivated variables $(p, \xi)$ rather than arbitrary dimensions,
gaining the exact divergence property as a structural bonus.

\paragraph{Thermostats in machine learning.}
Stochastic gradient Nos\'{e}--Hoover thermostat (SGNHT)
\citep{ding2014bayesian} uses NH dynamics with added noise for scalable Bayesian
inference. Thermostat-enhanced MCMC methods have been explored for molecular
simulation \citep{leimkuhler2013rational}. Colored-noise thermostats
\citep{ceriotti2010colored} generalize the NH framework to frequency-dependent
coupling via generalized Langevin equations. Our work differs by focusing on the
\emph{exact divergence} property for density evaluation, rather than on sampling
efficiency alone.

\paragraph{Score-based diffusion models.}
Score matching \citep{song2021score}, DDPM \citep{ho2020denoising}, and flow
matching \citep{lipman2023flow} define the current state of the art in generative
modeling. These methods learn a time-dependent score function
$s_\theta(x, t) \approx \nabla \log p_t(x)$. NH-CNF replaces this learned score
with a 1D thermostat variable $\xi$ --- a drastic dimensionality reduction that
is only possible because the dynamics are derived from a known potential $V(q)$.
NH-CNF is therefore complementary to, not competitive with, learned diffusion
models: it applies to settings where the energy function is available (energy-based
models, molecular simulation, Bayesian inference).

% ============================================================================
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================================

We have shown that the Nos\'{e}--Hoover thermostat, viewed as a continuous
normalizing flow, provides exact $O(1)$ divergence computation with zero variance.
This is a structural property of the dynamics --- not an approximation or a
learned behavior --- arising from the thermostat's role as a scalar feedback
controller for thermal equilibrium.

\paragraph{Where NH-CNF works well.}
The framework is best suited for three settings: (a)~known energy functions where
$\nabla V(q)$ is available (energy-based models, molecular dynamics, Bayesian
inference with explicit likelihoods), (b)~multimodal targets in moderate dimension
($d \lesssim 20$) where deterministic Hamiltonian trajectories cross barriers more
efficiently than random walks, and (c)~applications requiring deterministic,
reproducible density evaluation (importance sampling, model comparison, calibration
auditing).

\paragraph{Where it does not.}
NH-CNF is not applicable to raw image generation or other domains where the energy
function is not available --- a learned potential would reintroduce the need for
Hutchinson estimation. The deterministic trapping at $d > 20$
(Section~\ref{sec:scaling}) is a genuine limitation, not a tuning artifact. For
high-dimensional multimodal targets, stochastic methods remain essential.

\paragraph{The broader picture.}
The NH thermostat was invented 40 years ago to simulate molecules at constant
temperature. The exact divergence property was a known mathematical fact, not a
discovery. What we contribute is the recognition that this property solves a
concrete problem in modern generative modeling --- the variance and cost of trace
estimation in CNFs --- and systematic experimental evidence for where this
connection is useful and where it breaks down.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Hybrid deterministic-stochastic flows that periodically refresh momentum could
combine the exact divergence of NH dynamics with the ergodicity of stochastic
methods. Adaptive $Q$ schedules, potentially learned, could extend the framework
to higher dimensions. The connection between thermostat mass spectra and diffusion
noise schedules (Section~\ref{sec:multiscale}) suggests a principled way to design
sampling dynamics that deserves further investigation. Finally, applying NH-CNF to
molecular generation --- where the energy function is physically given and
dimensions are moderate --- is a natural next step.

\section{Full Derivation of the Exact Divergence}
\label{app:derivation}
% ============================================================================

We provide the complete derivation of Theorem~\ref{thm:exact_div} with all
intermediate steps, for readers unfamiliar with the Liouville equation formalism.

The NH dynamics define a vector field $f$ on the extended phase space
$\mathcal{M} = \R^d \times \R^d \times \R$ with coordinates
$(q_1, \ldots, q_d, p_1, \ldots, p_d, \xi)$:
\begin{equation}
  f = \bigl(\underbrace{p_1/m, \ldots, p_d/m}_{\dot{q}},\;
    \underbrace{-\partial_{q_1}V - g(\xi)p_1, \ldots,
      -\partial_{q_d}V - g(\xi)p_d}_{\dot{p}},\;
    \underbrace{(|p|^2/m - d\kT)/Q}_{\dot{\xi}}\bigr).
\end{equation}

The divergence is:
\begin{equation}
  \divergence(f) = \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial f_{q_i}}{\partial q_i}
    + \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial f_{p_i}}{\partial p_i}
    + \frac{\partial f_\xi}{\partial \xi}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Term 1: Position derivatives.}
$f_{q_i} = p_i / m$. Since $p_i$ and $m$ do not depend on $q_i$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial}{\partial q_i}\left(\frac{p_i}{m}\right) = 0
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial f_{q_i}}{\partial q_i} = 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Term 2: Momentum derivatives.}
$f_{p_i} = -\partial V / \partial q_i - g(\xi) \cdot p_i$. The first term
depends on $q$ only; the second is linear in $p_i$ with coefficient $-g(\xi)$:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial}{\partial p_i}\bigl(-\partial_{q_i}V - g(\xi)\,p_i\bigr)
    = 0 - g(\xi) = -g(\xi)
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  \sum_{i=1}^d \frac{\partial f_{p_i}}{\partial p_i} = -d \cdot g(\xi).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Term 3: Thermostat derivative.}
$f_\xi = (|p|^2/m - d\kT) / Q$. The numerator $|p|^2/m = \sum_i p_i^2/m$
depends on $p$, not on $\xi$. The denominator $Q$ is a constant. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial}{\partial \xi}\left(\frac{|p|^2/m - d\kT}{Q}\right) = 0
  \quad \Longrightarrow \quad
  \frac{\partial f_\xi}{\partial \xi} = 0.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Total.}
\begin{equation}
  \divergence(f) = 0 + (-d \cdot g(\xi)) + 0 = -d \cdot g(\xi). \qquad \qed
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Connection to the Liouville equation.}
The invariant measure $\rho \propto \exp(-H/\kT - Q\Phi(\xi)/\kT)$ satisfies
the Liouville equation $\partial_t \rho + \divergence(\rho f) = 0$ in steady
state. Expanding: $\divergence(\rho f) = \rho\,\divergence(f) + f \cdot \nabla\rho = 0$.
Using $\divergence(f) = -d\,g(\xi)$ and computing $f \cdot \nabla \rho / \rho$
yields $d\,g(\xi)$ after cancellation, confirming consistency.

\paragraph{Multi-thermostat extension.}
For $N$ parallel thermostats $\xi_1, \ldots, \xi_N$ with independent dynamics
(Equation~\ref{eq:multi_xi}), each adds a $-g(\xi_k)$ contribution per momentum
dimension and a zero contribution from its own $\partial \dot{\xi}_k / \partial \xi_k$.
The total divergence is $-d \cdot \sum_{k=1}^N g(\xi_k)$.

\section{$Q_{\mathrm{eff}}$ Universality: Friction Shape Does Not Matter}
\label{app:qeff}
% ============================================================================

A natural question is whether the choice of coupling function $g(\xi)$ matters for
sampling quality. We tested five functions --- $g(\xi) = \xi$ (classical NH),
$\tanh(\xi)$, $\mathrm{erf}(\xi)$, $\xi/(1+|\xi|)$ (soft clamp), and
$\sin(\xi)$ (periodic) --- on the 2D double-well benchmark.

The key finding is that sampling quality depends on $g$ only through the
\emph{effective thermostat mass}:
\begin{equation}
  Q_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{Q}{g'(0)}.
  \label{eq:qeff}
\end{equation}
When $Q_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is matched across friction functions, all choices produce
statistically identical KL divergence, energy distance, and autocorrelation time.
The linearization around the equilibrium point $\xi = 0$ (where the thermostat
spends most of its time for well-equilibrated systems) determines the effective
coupling strength; the nonlinear behavior at large $|\xi|$ is visited rarely and
contributes negligibly to long-time averages.

This universality has a practical consequence: the choice of $g$ can be made for
numerical convenience (bounded $\tanh$ for stability) without affecting sampling
quality, as long as $Q$ is adjusted to maintain the desired $Q_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

For the exact divergence property, the choice of $g$ is immaterial: the formula
$\divergence(f) = -d \cdot g(\xi)$ holds for any differentiable $g$ with
$g(0) = 0$.

\section{Experimental Details}
\label{app:details}
% ============================================================================

\paragraph{Integration.}
All experiments use velocity Verlet splitting adapted for NH dynamics. The
integration scheme splits the NH flow into three substeps per time step:
(1)~half-step thermostat update $\xi \leftarrow \xi + (\Delta t/2)\dot{\xi}$,
(2)~full-step Hamiltonian update $(q, p) \leftarrow \text{Verlet}(q, p, \Delta t)$
with friction $g(\xi)$, (3)~half-step thermostat update. This preserves
time-reversibility (up to the friction term) and provides second-order accuracy.

\paragraph{Langevin baseline.}
ULA with step size $\epsilon = 0.005$, 8000 steps. For the BNN experiment, SGLD

\paragraph{Burn-in and thinning.}
For sampling experiments (E1, E6, E7), we discard the first 20\% of steps as
burn-in and thin every 50th sample. For the BNN (E2), burn-in is 50\% and

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
Energy distance is computed using the standard formula
$\text{ED} = 2\E[\|X - Y\|] - \E[\|X - X'\|] - \E[\|Y - Y'\|]$ where $X$ are
model samples and $Y$ are ground-truth samples. Test log-likelihood in E7 is
computed by fitting a Gaussian KDE (bandwidth selected by Scott's rule) on the
sampler output and evaluating on held-out test points.

\paragraph{Software.}
All experiments are implemented in Python using NumPy and SciPy. No GPU
acceleration is used --- all computation is on CPU, which is appropriate for the
low-dimensional problems studied here. The gradient $\nabla V(q)$ is computed
analytically for the GMM targets and via finite differences for the KDE-fitted
potentials.
